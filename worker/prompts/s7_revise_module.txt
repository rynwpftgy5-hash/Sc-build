## Revision rules

- **Surgical fixes only.** Address EVERY issue named in the S7 rationale and
  EVERY failed claim. Do NOT rewrite unaffected passages. The vast majority
  of the transcript should pass through unchanged.
- **Preserve audio-native voice.** The original is a spoken-audio script
  with conversational pacing, listener-second-person stance, pacing cues
  ("here's the key bit"), and in-line source naming. Match that register.
- **Replace failed claims with verifiable alternatives.** Use a different
  source from CITATIONS_JSON if one fits better. If no available source
  supports the underlying claim, REMOVE the claim and trim the surrounding
  beat — don't paper over it with vaguer language. Better to drop a wrong
  claim than to soften it into ambiguity.
- **Correct phantom attributions surgically — do NOT go vague.** The
  failure mode to AVOID is replacing "Per the CFA Institute's framework
  on X, …" with "Analysts widely view X as …" — that's trading one
  phantom (named source that doesn't exist) for another (unspecified
  group consensus that's equally unverifiable). The judge will flag the
  new vague phrasing just as hard as the old phantom citation.

  Three GOOD ways to fix a phantom attribution, in order of preference:

  1. **Replace with a real source from CITATIONS_JSON.** If the CFA
     Institute attribution is phantom but the underlying claim is real,
     find a source in CITATIONS_JSON that actually supports it. "Per the
     SEC's filing requirements under Regulation S-K, …" is far better
     than "analysts widely…"

  2. **Reframe as descriptive practice WITHOUT authority claim.** Use
     "what analysts call X" or "the X framework — informally" or simply
     present the concept without attribution at all. The phrase "what
     analysts call the four-question framework" is fine — it describes
     a practice without claiming a named authority backs it. "Analysts
     widely use" is NOT fine — it's a covert authority claim.

  3. **Drop the claim entirely** if neither (1) nor (2) works. Remove
     the sentence and reshape the surrounding beat to flow without it.
     Better to lose a beat than to preserve a vague claim that fails
     verification again.

  Concrete before/after:

  ❌ Bad fix: "Per the CFA Institute's analyst framework, the five core
  metrics are …" → "Analysts widely agree the five core metrics are …"
  (still vague-attribution)

  ✓ Good fix #1: "Per the CFA Institute's analyst framework…" → "Per
  CFA Institute Level II curriculum materials on equity analysis, …"
  (real specific source from CITATIONS_JSON)

  ✓ Good fix #2: "Per the CFA Institute's analyst framework, the five
  core metrics are …" → "The five metrics most analysts watch first are
  …" (descriptive, no authority claim)

  ✓ Good fix #3: Cut "Per the CFA Institute's analyst framework, the
  five core metrics are X, Y, Z" entirely and replace the beat with a
  worked example that demonstrates one or two metrics in action — no
  framework claim needed.
- **Preserve every citation that wasn't flagged.** All inline source
  mentions in passages NOT touched by revision must remain identical.
- **Length discipline.** Revised transcript should be within ±15% of the
  original word count. If you find yourself wanting to add a long
  digression, trim elsewhere.

## What you're NOT doing

- Not regenerating the whole module from scratch.
- Not adding new pedagogical material the listener didn't ask for.
- Not changing the learning objective or the structural arc (hook → core
  → sub-concepts → integration → self-checks).
- Not fixing stylistic issues that weren't flagged. Don't gold-plate.
