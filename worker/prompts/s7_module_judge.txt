## Judgment criteria

### 1. Cross-source consistency
Do any two cited sources contradict each other on a substantive point as
used in this module? Example: one source defines "operating margin" one
way and another source defines it differently, and both definitions are
treated as authoritative side by side. Note that two sources offering
different *framings* of the same fact (e.g. SEC procedural view vs CFA
analytical view) is NOT contradiction — it's complementary perspective.

### 2. Internal consistency
Do any two claims within the module contradict each other? Example: an
early beat says "deferred revenue is a liability" and a later beat treats
it as an asset.

### 3. Pedagogical soundness
- Does the module actually teach the stated learning_objective?
- Are concepts introduced before they're used?
- Does each sub-concept beat advance the listener toward the objective,
  or does any beat drift off-topic?
- Are the self-checks answerable from the module alone?

## Verdict

**DEFAULT TO `approved` WHEN THE MODULE FUNDAMENTALLY WORKS.** This judge is
the gate to the next stage, not a fault-finding exercise. The bar is "would
a thoughtful listener walk away having genuinely learned what the objective
promised?" — not "is this textbook-perfect."

- **approved** — All three dimensions FUNDAMENTALLY work. Minor stylistic,
  pacing, or phrasing concerns are fine; surface them in `concerns[]` but
  still return `approved`. Use this verdict when the module teaches the
  objective and the listener would not be misled.
- **revise** — There's a CONCRETE defect that materially undermines the
  module's purpose: a substantive factual error, a missing concept the
  objective requires, internal contradiction, or pedagogy that fails the
  listener. Reserve this for problems that would actually warrant the cost
  of re-running drafting. Not for "this could be tighter" or "I'd phrase
  this differently."
- **reject** — Fundamental problem: objective unteachable with available
  sources, sources too thin to support claims, or module is incoherent at
  the structural level. Should not enter the library even after revision.

Calibration check: if you find yourself marking `revise` with only
stylistic, organizational, or phrasing concerns — that's `approved` with
notes. If you find yourself marking `approved` despite a real factual or
pedagogical error — that's `revise`. Be honest about the distinction.
